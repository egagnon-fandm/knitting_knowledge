# Poincloux — Knit and Stretch (Physics World, 2019)

**Summary**: Popular science article describing the physics of knitted fabrics — the loop-based deformation mechanism, the contrast with woven textiles, the nylon monofilament model system, and the two-part elastic + crackling mechanical response.

## Citation

Poincloux, S. (2019). Knit and stretch. *Physics World*, January 2019.

## Main themes

### Knit vs. weave

Knitted fabrics are built from a single continuous yarn looped through itself row by row. Woven fabrics are built from two perpendicular sets of yarns crossing over and under each other.

The key mechanical difference: when a woven fabric is stretched, the yarns themselves must be stretched or compressed. In a knitted fabric, the loops can unfurl and reorient geometrically — the yarn undergoes bending and reorientation far more than stretching. This is why knits are so much more extensible than wovens: elongations of 200–300% are possible without plastically deforming the yarn, while wovens typically extend only a few percent elastically.

### Nylon monofilament as model system

The Poincloux group (PRX 2018, PRL 2018) used nylon fishing line (monofilament) rather than commercial spun yarn because:
- Homogeneous, reproducible cross-section
- No intra-yarn friction (single filament)
- Well-characterized elastic properties
- Allows direct comparison with first-principles models

The 83×83 stitch samples used in the crackling dynamics study ([[poincloux-prl-2018]]) were made from this material.

### Two-part mechanical response

The elastic response of a knitted fabric has two regimes:
1. **Geometric (bending-dominated)**: loops reorient and unfurl; the fabric extends at low force. This is nonlinear — the stress-strain curve is concave.
2. **Stretching-dominated**: once loops are straightened, yarn itself must stretch. The stress-strain curve becomes steeper.

This J-shape behavior is described in quantitative detail in [[poincloux-prx-2018]] and [[singal-naturecomm-2024]].

### Crackling

On top of the average elastic response, the fabric emits audible crackling sounds during stretching. These arise from sudden jumps in loop geometry — stick-slip events at yarn crossings — which propagate as avalanches. The statistics follow a power law with exponent ~−3/2. See [[crackling-dynamics]] and [[poincloux-prl-2018]].

### Validity range

The first-principles elastic model (from [[poincloux-prx-2018]]) is valid up to approximately 2× elongation (100% strain). Beyond this, yarn stretching becomes important and the model deviates.
