# Crane — A Simple Parametric Model of Plain-Knit Yarns (2023)

**Summary**: A short technical note giving closed-form parametric expressions for the plain-knit yarn centerline and twisted fiber strands, suitable for appearance modeling and previsualisation. The model is geometric rather than physical but efficient to evaluate, and is used as the FEA geometry source in du Pasquier et al. 2025.

**Sources**: `raw/YarnCurve.pdf`

**Last updated**: 2026-05-15

## Citation

Crane, K. (2023). A simple parametric model of plain-knit yarns. Technical note, March 29, 2023.

## Yarn centerline

The plain-knit yarn centerline is a smooth curve $\gamma : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^3$:

$$\boxed{\gamma(t) := (t + a\sin(2t),\; h\cos(t),\; d\cos(2t))}$$

| Parameter | Role |
|---|---|
| $a$ | Loop roundness — controls how much the yarn "overshoots" laterally at each loop |
| $h > 0$ | Loop height — controls the wale-direction amplitude |
| $d > 0$ | Depth oscillation — controls how far the yarn goes in and out of the fabric plane |

A single period $t \in [0, 2\pi]$ traces one complete stitch loop. Multiple rows are assembled by offsetting in the wale ($y$) direction; a snug fit between adjacent rows is achieved with row spacing $w = h + \tfrac{1}{2}$ (source: YarnCurve.pdf §4).

Typical rendering parameters: $a = 3/2$, $h = 4$, $d = 1$, tube radius $R = 4/5$.

## Yarn fiber strands (marled yarn)

To model the twisted individual fibers within the yarn, a fiber curve winding around $\gamma(t)$ is defined using the Frenet frame $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$ of $\gamma$:

$$\eta_\phi(t) := \gamma(t) + r\!\left(\cos(\theta_\phi(t))\,e_2(t) + \sin(\theta_\phi(t))\,e_3(t)\right)$$

where $r$ is the yarn thickness and $\theta_\phi(t) = \omega t - 2\cos(t) + \phi$.

The $-2\cos(t)$ term approximately corrects for the non-uniform speed of rotation of the Frenet frame (a proxy for the Bishop / parallel-transport frame, which requires global integration). Without this correction, fiber twists appear uneven along the loop. Multiple fibers are generated by varying the phase offset $\phi$.

## Arc length

The arc length element of $\gamma(t)$ is:

$$ds_\gamma = \sqrt{(1 + 2a\cos(2t))^2 + h^2\sin^2(t) + 4d^2\sin^2(2t)}\;dt$$

This integral does not have a closed form, so yarn length per loop is estimated numerically over $t \in [0, 2\pi]$ (source: YarnCurve.pdf §4).

## Tube thickness

The maximum tube radius $R$ such that a disk of radius $R$ swept perpendicularly along $\gamma$ yields no self-intersections equals the **global radius of curvature** (Gonzalez & Maddocks 1999). There is no simple closed-form expression; the *octrope* algorithm can compute it from a polygon approximation (source: YarnCurve.pdf §4).

## Relationship to other models

- **Peirce 1947** ([[peirce-1947]]): earliest parametric stitch model (2D, piecewise circular); lacks curvature continuity and fiber parameterization.
- **Demiroz & Dias 2000** ([[demiroz-2000]]): piecewise cubic spline with 5 control points; no fiber model.
- **Wadekar et al. 2020** ([[wadekar-2020]]): helicoid scaffold; captures yarn twist but requires optimization.
- **du Pasquier et al. 2025** ([[du-pasquier-2025]]): uses Crane 2023 directly as the centerline geometry for their volumetric FEA model; stitch arc lengths (3.9–5.9 mm) measured from microscopy and fitted to $\gamma$.

Crane's model is purely geometric — it does not minimize a physical energy. The curve shape arises from trigonometric construction rather than bending/contact equilibrium. It is therefore best used as an initial condition or visualization tool, not as a mechanical prediction.
